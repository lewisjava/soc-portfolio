# Digital Forensics Fundamentals

Forensics is the application of methods and procedures to investigate and solve crimes. Digital forensics applies this to cyber crime — any crime committed on or using a digital device. Several tools and techniques are used to investigate digital devices thoroughly after an incident to find and analyse evidence.

## Methodology

**Collection → Examination → Analysis → Reporting**

- **Collection** — identifying all devices from which data can be collected. Investigators typically find personal computers, laptops, cameras, USB drives, phones, and more. It's essential to ensure the original data is not tampered with during collection.
- **Examination** — the collected data can overwhelm investigators, so it needs to be filtered and the data of interest extracted for the next stage.
- **Analysis** — the critical phase. Data is analysed by correlating multiple pieces of evidence to draw conclusions. This depends heavily on the case scenario and available data, and aims to extract relevant activities in chronological order.
- **Reporting** — the final phase. A detailed report is produced, covering the investigation methodology, findings from the collected evidence, and potentially recommendations.

## Types of digital forensics

- **Computer forensics** — the most common type, investigating computers.
- **Mobile forensics** — investigating mobile devices, extracting data like GPS location, texts, and call records.
- **Network forensics** — investigating the network, with the majority of evidence found in network traffic logs.
- **Database forensics** — investigating intrusions into databases that result in data modification or exfiltration.
- **Cloud forensics** — investigating data stored on cloud infrastructure. This can be tricky since there's often little evidence readily available on cloud platforms.
- **Email forensics** — email remains the most common professional communication method, making it an important part of digital forensics.
